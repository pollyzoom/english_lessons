Introduction

No one knows exactly why music has such a profoundvery deep, strong / глубокий effect on our emotions. But thanks to scientific studies, we have some exciting clueshints, evidence / подсказки.

The expectation theory

The most popular theory among scientists goes back to 1956. Philosopher and composer Leonard Meyer suggested that emotion in music is all about what we expectthink will happen / ожидать and whether we get it. Meyer used earlier psychological theories which said that emotions appear when we cannot satisfyfulfill, make happy / удовлетворить some desire. This creates frustration or anger, but if we finally find what we're looking for — whether it's love or a piece of chocolate — the rewardprize, good result / награда feels even sweeter.

This, Meyer proposed, is what music does. It creates sonic patterns that make us unconsciouslywithout realising / бессознательно predict what's coming next. If we're right, the brain gives itself a little reward — a surgesudden increase / всплеск of dopaminehappiness chemical / дофамин. The constant dance between expectation and outcome fills the brain with a pleasurable mix of emotions.

Fun fact: Dopamine is the same chemical your brain releases when you eat your favourite food or get a great mark at school!
Brain science

In 2001, neuroscientists Anne Blood and Robert Zatorre at McGill University in Montreal used brain scanning to prove that people listening to pleasurableenjoyable / приятный music activate special brain regions called the limbicbrain's emotion centre / лимбическая система and paralimbic areas. These areas are connected to euphoricextremely happy / эйфорический reward responses — similar to the good feelings we get from delicious food or achieving a goal.

The mystery continues

But why does a sequenceordered series / последовательность of sounds with no obvious survival valueimportance for staying alive / ценность для выживания do the same thing? The truth is no one knows for sure. However, we now have many clues.

We expect rising melodies to continue to rise — but not forever. We expect pleasing harmoniespleasant note combinations / гармонии rather than jarring dissonanceunpleasant clash of sounds / резкий диссонанс — but what sounds nice today may have seemed unpleasant two hundred years ago. We expect rhythms to be regular but are surprised when the jumpy syncopationoff-beat rhythm / синкопа of rock'n'roll suddenly switches to a completely different beat.

We can all agree: music is all around us, regulating our moods and bringing good feelings to those who listen. It raises our spirits, brings excitement, or calms us down.
